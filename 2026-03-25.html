Wednesday, Living in This World but Not of It

Colossians · Week 13, Day 5
March 25, 2026
✦ ☩ ✦
📌 TL;DR

Two names, one verse, two completely different endings: Luke stayed, Demas left, and Paul lets the silence between them say everything.

  • ☩ Paul names Luke and Demas side by side in Colossians 4:14, both apparently faithful coworkers at this point in the story. Years later, in 2 Timothy 4:10-11, the contrast becomes devastating.
  • ☩ Demas deserted Paul because he "loved this present world," while Luke remained the only companion standing with Paul at the end of his life.
  • ☩ The passage also introduces Nympha, a woman whose house served as a church, reminding us that faithfulness often looks less like fame and more like opening your front door.
  • ☩ The passage asks us to examine what we love more: the comfort the world offers today, or the kingdom we are building for eternity.
🔁 Connection to Yesterday

Yesterday we watched Epaphras wrestle in prayer for three cities he could not visit, agonizing before God on behalf of people he loved from a prison cell 1,200 miles away. Today we meet two more names from that same Roman prison: Luke the physician who stayed, and Demas who would eventually leave. Epaphras showed us what faithfulness looks like in prayer. Today's passage shows us what faithfulness and its opposite look like over the long arc of a life.

🗺️ Historical & Cultural Context
The Physician, the Deserter, and the Woman With the Open Door

Luke is one of the most remarkable figures in the New Testament, and Paul introduces him with a single phrase that tells us almost everything: "the dearly loved physician." Luke was a Gentile, one of the only non-Jewish authors in the entire Bible, and his medical training would have made him an invaluable companion to a man whose body bore the scars of beatings, stonings, and shipwrecks. But Luke was more than Paul's doctor. He was the author of both the Gospel of Luke and the book of Acts, two volumes that together make up roughly 27% of the New Testament. The careful, detailed historical writing that characterizes Luke-Acts was the work of a man who traveled with Paul, observed firsthand, and documented the movement of the gospel across the Roman world with the precision of a trained scientist.

Demas, by contrast, appears in only three New Testament verses, and his trajectory is a slow-motion departure. In Philemon 24, he is listed among Paul's "coworkers." Here in Colossians 4:14, he sends greetings but receives no title, no commendation, no description. And in 2 Timothy 4:10, written during Paul's final imprisonment, the verdict falls: "Demas has deserted me, since he loved this present world." The Greek phrase is agapesas ton nun aiona, "having loved the present age." The same word used for God's love for the world in John 3:16 is used here for Demas's love of the world's comforts. Love is not neutral. It always attaches to something.

📖 Into the Word

Today's anchor passage is Colossians 4:14-15, where Paul names Luke, Demas, and Nympha. The supporting passage from 2 Timothy 4:10-11 reveals the dramatic contrast that only time would expose, and 1 John 2:15 provides the theological framework for understanding what went wrong with Demas. Four additional passages (Mark 13:32-37, Titus 2:11-14, 2 Peter 3:10-14, and Revelation 3:17-21) counsel believers on how to live faithfully while awaiting Christ's return, forming the positive counterpart to Demas's cautionary tale.

Colossians 4:14-15
☩ Open in Bible App
"Luke, the dearly loved physician, and Demas send you greetings. Give my greetings to the brothers and sisters in Laodicea, and to Nympha and the church in her home."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
The asymmetry in Paul's descriptions is striking and, in hindsight, ominous. Luke receives a warm title: "the dearly loved physician" (ho iatros ho agapetos). Demas receives nothing. No title. No commendation. No adjective. Just his name. When you compare this to the rich descriptions Paul gave Tychicus ("dearly loved brother, faithful minister, fellow servant"), Onesimus ("faithful and dearly loved brother"), and Epaphras ("servant of Christ Jesus, always wrestling in prayer"), the silence around Demas is deafening. Paul may not have known yet what would happen, but the absence of warmth in the description is notable. Then Paul pivots to Nympha (or Nymphas, depending on manuscript tradition), a person in Laodicea whose house served as a gathering place for the church. House churches were the norm in the first century; purpose-built church buildings did not exist until the third century. Nympha's hospitality was not a side ministry. It was the ministry. Without people willing to open their homes, there was no church.
2 Timothy 4:10-11
☩ Open in Bible App
"because Demas has deserted me, since he loved this present world, and has gone to Thessalonica. Crescens has gone to Galatia, Titus to Dalmatia. Only Luke is with me. Bring Mark with you, for he is useful to me in the ministry."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Written years after Colossians, during Paul's second and final Roman imprisonment, these verses complete the stories that began in today's anchor passage. Demas, the man who sent greetings in Colossians 4:14 with no commendation, has now formally deserted. The verb egkataleipo means to abandon, to leave behind in the lurch. Paul names the cause with surgical precision: he loved this present world. Not a dramatic heresy. Not a theological crisis. Just a gradual gravitational pull toward comfort, safety, and the pleasures available right now. Thessalonica was a prosperous, cosmopolitan city. Demas traded a prison cell for a thriving metropolis. Meanwhile, Luke, the dearly loved physician, is the last man standing. "Only Luke is with me." Of all the names Paul has listed across Colossians, Philemon, and his other letters, only one person remained at the end. Faithfulness is not measured in a single moment but over the full length of a life.
1 John 2:15
☩ Open in Bible App
"Do not love the world or the things in the world. If anyone loves the world, the love of the Father is not in him."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
John's warning is not about geography or culture; it is about allegiance. The "world" (kosmos) here refers to the organized system of values, pleasures, and priorities that operates apart from God. John does not say the world is unreal or unimportant. He says it competes for the same space in your heart that the Father occupies. You cannot love both with full devotion. Demas discovered this the hard way. His story is not the story of a dramatic fall from faith. It is the story of a slow drift, a quiet rearrangement of priorities, until one day the pull of "this present world" was stronger than the pull of the kingdom. That is what makes his story so terrifying: it can happen to anyone, not in a single catastrophic decision but in a thousand small ones.

Mark 13:32-37
☩ Open in Bible App
"Now concerning that day or hour no one knows, neither the angels in heaven nor the Son, but only the Father. Watch! Be alert! For you don't know when the time is coming. It is like a man on a journey, who left his house, gave authority to his servants, gave each one his work, and commanded the doorkeeper to be alert. Therefore be alert, since you don't know when the master of the house is coming, whether in the evening or at midnight or at the crowing of the rooster or early in the morning. Otherwise, when he comes suddenly he might find you sleeping. And what I say to you, I say to everyone: Be alert!"
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Jesus's repeated command to "be alert" (gregoreite) is the opposite of Demas's trajectory. Demas fell asleep spiritually, lulled by the comforts of the present age until the pull of Thessalonica was stronger than the pull of the kingdom. Jesus warns that the master's return will be sudden and unexpected, and the only preparation is constant vigilance. The doorkeeper does not know the schedule; he only knows his assignment: stay awake.
Titus 2:11-14
☩ Open in Bible App
"For the grace of God has appeared, bringing salvation for all people, instructing us to deny godlessness and worldly lusts and to live in a sensible, righteous, and godly way in the present age, while we wait for the blessed hope, the appearing of the glory of our great God and Savior, Jesus Christ. He gave himself for us to redeem us from all lawlessness and to cleanse for himself a people for his own possession, eager to do good works."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Paul's letter to Titus provides the positive counterpart to Demas's negative example. Grace does not just save us; it instructs us. It teaches us to deny "worldly lusts" (the very thing Demas could not deny) and to live with self-control, righteousness, and godliness "in the present age" (the very age Demas chose to love). The phrase "while we wait for the blessed hope" describes the posture of a believer who lives fully in the present without being owned by it. This is the Luke posture: present, faithful, serving, and waiting.
2 Peter 3:10-14
☩ Open in Bible App
"But the day of the Lord will come like a thief; on that day the heavens will pass away with a loud noise, the elements will burn and be dissolved, and the earth and the works on it will be disclosed. Since all these things are to be dissolved in this way, it is clear what sort of people you should be in holy conduct and godliness, as you wait for the day of God and hasten its coming. ... But based on his promise, we wait for new heavens and a new earth, where righteousness dwells. Therefore, dear friends, while you wait for these things, make every effort to be found without spot or blemish in his sight, at peace."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Peter's logic is devastating in its simplicity: if everything in the present world is temporary, then the present world is a terrible thing to build your life around. The "elements" that will be dissolved include every comfort, every career, every city that Demas traded the kingdom for. The question Peter raises is not whether the present world is real or enjoyable; it is whether it is worth your ultimate allegiance. "What sort of people you should be" is not a guilt trip but an invitation to align your investments with the only reality that will survive the fire.
Revelation 3:17-21
☩ Open in Bible App
"For you say, 'I'm rich; I have become wealthy and need nothing,' and you don't realize that you are wretched, pitiful, poor, blind, and naked. I advise you to buy from me gold refined in the fire so that you may be rich, white clothes so that you may be dressed and your shameful nakedness not be exposed, and ointment to spread on your eyes so that you may see. As many as I love, I rebuke and discipline. So be zealous and repent. See! I stand at the door and knock. If anyone hears my voice and opens the door, I will come in to him and eat with him, and he with me. To the one who conquers I will give the right to sit with me on my throne, just as I also conquered and sat down with my Father on his throne."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Jesus gives Laodicea a three-part prescription that directly addresses the three things they are most proud of. You think you are wealthy? Buy real gold from Me, refined in fire. You think you are clothed in fine black wool? Put on My white garments. You think your famous eye salve gives you sight? Let Me anoint your eyes so you can truly see. Jesus does not approach Laodicea with destruction. He approaches with discipline ("as many as I love, I rebuke"), an invitation to repent, and the stunning image of Himself standing at the door, knocking. The God of the universe does not break in; He waits to be invited. And the promise for those who open the door is not merely forgiveness but intimacy: "I will come in to him and eat with him." This is the invitation that makes all the difference between the Demas trajectory and the Luke trajectory. Will you open the door?
✝️ The Christ Connection

Jesus faced the same temptation Demas faced and made the opposite choice. In the wilderness, Satan offered Him the kingdoms of this world, right now, no suffering required (Matthew 4:8-10). On the night before the cross, Jesus asked the Father if there was another way, and then submitted to the way that led through death (Matthew 26:39). Demas loved this present world. Jesus loved the world so much that He gave Himself for it. The difference is the direction of the love. Demas's love pulled him away from the mission. Christ's love drove Him deeper into it. And because Christ stayed when every human instinct said to leave, we have a Savior who will never desert us, even when we have deserted Him.

✅ Personal Application Challenge
Take five minutes today and honestly list the three things in your life that most compete for your heart's allegiance with God. They are probably not evil things. They might be comfort, career advancement, financial security, social approval, or entertainment. Name them specifically. Then ask yourself: if staying faithful to God's call required releasing one of these, which would be the hardest to let go? That is your Thessalonica. Pray over it today.
🙏 Closing Prayer

Lord, we do not want to be Demas. We do not want to send greetings today and send excuses tomorrow. But we are honest enough to admit that the pull of this present world is real, and it does not always come with a warning label. Show us the quiet subscriptions running in the background of our hearts, the small allegiances that are slowly redirecting our love away from You. Give us the steadfastness of Luke, who was still in the room when everyone else had left. And thank You, Jesus, that You stayed. You stayed through the wilderness, through the garden, through the cross. You never deserted us. Help us live like people who remember that. In Jesus' name, Amen.

💬 Discussion Question
Demas did not leave the faith in a single dramatic moment but through a gradual drift. What are the early warning signs that someone's love for the present world is beginning to outweigh their love for the kingdom?
