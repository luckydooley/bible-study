📌 TL;DR

Paul's inner circle was a collection of people who should not have worked together, and that was exactly the point.

  • ☩ Paul names three Jewish coworkers in these verses: Aristarchus the fellow prisoner, Mark the once-rejected deserter, and Jesus called Justus, an otherwise unknown believer who simply showed up and was faithful.
  • ☩ These three are described as Paul's "only" Jewish coworkers for the kingdom, a note that carries both gratitude and a trace of sadness about the broader Jewish Christian community's lack of support.
  • ☩ Mark's presence in this list is a quiet earthquake: the man Paul once refused to take on a mission trip is now endorsed to the Colossian church as someone worth welcoming.
  • ☩ The greetings reveal that Paul's network crossed every social boundary in the Roman world, and the gospel's credibility depended on exactly that kind of unity.
🔁 Connection to Yesterday

Yesterday we met Tychicus and Onesimus, the two men Paul chose to carry the Colossian letter from Rome. Today the camera widens to reveal the rest of the team standing behind them. If Tychicus and Onesimus were the messengers, Aristarchus, Mark, and Jesus Justus were the support system, the Jewish believers who stuck with Paul when many others did not. Together, these names paint a picture of a community built not on shared ethnicity or background but on shared commitment to the gospel.

🗺️ Historical & Cultural Context
The Fracture Line Running Through the Early Church

The tension between Jewish and Gentile believers was not a minor disagreement in the early church. It was the defining fault line. The Jerusalem Council in Acts 15 had formally ruled that Gentile converts did not need to be circumcised, but rulings do not change hearts overnight. Jewish Christians who had grown up keeping Torah their entire lives struggled to eat at the same table with Gentiles who had grown up eating pork and worshiping idols. The social pressure was enormous on both sides. Jewish believers who associated too closely with Gentile converts risked being ostracized by their own synagogue communities. Gentile believers who adopted Jewish customs risked losing their own cultural identity.

This is the backdrop for Paul's painful aside in verse 11: "These alone of the circumcised are my coworkers for the kingdom of God." That word "alone" (monoi in Greek) carries weight. Paul is not making a demographic observation. He is acknowledging a wound. Of all the Jewish Christians in Rome, only three were actively partnering with him in his Gentile-focused mission. The rest had either distanced themselves or were working at cross purposes. The fact that Paul names these three with such warmth reveals how much their loyalty cost them and how much it meant to him.

📖 Into the Word

Today's anchor passage is Colossians 4:10-11, where Paul introduces the three Jewish members of his team. The supporting passages from Ephesians 6:10-11 and Acts 15:36-40 illuminate Aristarchus's warrior identity and the dramatic backstory behind Mark's presence in this list. Together, they reveal a team forged not by convenience but by grace.

Colossians 4:10-11
☩ Open in Bible App
"Aristarchus, my fellow prisoner, sends you greetings, as does Mark, Barnabas's cousin (concerning whom you have received instructions: if he comes to you, welcome him), and so does Jesus who is called Justus. These alone of the circumcised are my coworkers for the kingdom of God, and they have been a comfort to me."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Paul introduces each man with precision. Aristarchus is called sunaichmalotos, literally "fellow war-captive," a term that could mean he was physically imprisoned alongside Paul or that he had voluntarily confined himself to serve Paul in prison. Either way, the word carries military overtones: Aristarchus was not just a friend; he was a soldier who had been captured alongside his commander. We know from Acts 19:29 that Aristarchus was seized by the Ephesian mob during the silversmith riot, and from Acts 27:2 that he accompanied Paul on the harrowing voyage to Rome that ended in shipwreck. This man had been physically dragged into danger for the gospel and kept showing up. Then comes Mark, introduced carefully: "Barnabas's cousin." Paul is connecting Mark to a name the Colossians would trust. But more striking is the parenthetical instruction: "if he comes to you, welcome him." The Colossians had apparently received prior instructions about Mark, possibly cautionary ones. Paul is overriding those instructions. The man who deserted the first missionary journey in Acts 13:13, whose departure later caused such a sharp disagreement between Paul and Barnabas that they split up entirely (Acts 15:39), is now publicly endorsed. Paul does not explain what changed. He simply declares the new reality. Finally, Jesus called Justus is someone we know nothing else about. No dramatic backstory. No famous relative. Just a Jewish believer who worked alongside Paul and brought him comfort. Sometimes faithfulness looks like showing up when nobody remembers your name.
Ephesians 6:10-11
☩ Open in Bible App
"Finally, be strengthened by the Lord and by his vast strength. Put on the full armor of God so that you can stand against the schemes of the devil."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
The military language Paul uses for Aristarchus ("fellow prisoner-of-war") echoes the spiritual warfare imagery Paul develops in Ephesians 6. Paul describes believers as soldiers fighting with "the armor of God" (Ephesians 6:13) against spiritual forces, and Aristarchus embodied this. He was both a spiritual warrior and a literal casualty of the mission, seized by mobs and shipwrecked on open water. Paul's description of him as a "fellow prisoner" is not just a status update; it is a commendation. In Paul's mind, sharing his chains was the highest form of solidarity.
Acts 15:37-39
☩ Open in Bible App
"Barnabas wanted to take along John Mark. But Paul insisted that they should not take along this man who had deserted them in Pamphylia and had not gone on with them to the work. They had such a sharp disagreement that they parted company, and Barnabas took Mark with him and sailed off to Cyprus."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
This is the backstory that makes Colossians 4:10 so remarkable. The Greek word for "sharp disagreement" is paroxusmos, from which we get "paroxysm." This was not a polite difference of opinion; it was an eruption. Paul felt so strongly about Mark's desertion that he split with Barnabas, his closest ministry partner, rather than risk bringing Mark again. And yet, years later, here is Mark, named among Paul's inner circle in Rome. Even more telling, in 2 Timothy 4:11, written near the end of Paul's life, he would write: "Bring Mark with you, for he is useful to me in the ministry." The arc from "deserter" to "useful" is one of the most quietly powerful redemption stories in the New Testament.
🎯 Street Level
The Group Project Nobody Wanted

Think about the worst group project you have ever been assigned. The one where the professor randomly grouped together people with completely different work styles, communication habits, and levels of commitment. Now imagine that one member quit halfway through, another got arrested, and a third person did solid work but nobody ever learned his name. That is basically Paul's team. Aristarchus: arrested. Mark: quit and came back. Jesus Justus: steady but invisible. And yet Paul describes this mismatched crew as the people who "have been a comfort to me." He does not say they were easy to work with. He does not say they were the dream team. He says they brought him comfort. Sometimes the people God puts around you are not the ones you would have chosen, and that is precisely what makes the team a testimony. If everyone on your team looked and thought exactly like you, nobody would notice the gospel at work. The friction, the forgiveness, the showing up anyway: that is the evidence.

🌍 Today's World, Ancient Truth
The Uncomfortable Advantage of Mixed Teams

Researchers at the University of Texas at Austin and Singapore set up simulated financial markets where participants bought and sold stocks in either same-race groups or ethnically diverse groups. The results, published in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, revealed something counterintuitive. The same-race groups felt more comfortable and trusted each other more quickly. But that trust became a liability: participants copied each other's mistakes, reinforced each other's blind spots, and created price bubbles. The diverse groups, by contrast, experienced more friction and disagreement, but they processed facts more carefully, caught errors faster, and made significantly better decisions.

Paul's team in Colossians 4 was a case study in this principle two millennia before anyone had the data. His inner circle included Jews and Gentiles, a former slave and free citizens, a man who had abandoned the mission and a man who had been imprisoned for it. This was not a comfortable team. But Paul was not building for comfort. He was building for the kingdom of God, and the kingdom has always advanced fastest when its workers are diverse enough to challenge each other and unified enough to keep moving forward together. The discomfort is not a bug; it is a feature.

✝️ The Christ Connection

Mark's story is a gospel story in miniature. He failed publicly. He was written off by the most influential leader in the early church. And then he was restored, not because he earned it but because grace made room for a second chance. This is exactly what Christ does. The one who denied knowing Jesus three times became the rock on which the church was built (John 21:15-17). The one who persecuted the church became its greatest missionary. And the one who deserted the first missionary journey went on to write the Gospel of Mark, the first written account of Jesus' life. Christ does not discard failures. He restores them, renames them, and sends them back into the work. Every member of Paul's team in these verses is living proof that the gospel does not require a perfect resume; it requires a willing heart.

✅ Personal Application Challenge
Is there a "Mark" in your life: someone who let you down, whom you have written off or kept at a distance? Today, take one step toward reopening that door. It does not have to be a full reconciliation conversation. It could be as simple as sending a message, praying for them by name, or mentally releasing the verdict you have been carrying. Paul's willingness to publicly endorse the person who once failed him changed the trajectory of the early church. Your willingness to do the same could change someone's trajectory too.
🙏 Closing Prayer

Lord, thank You for the uncomfortable teams, the mismatched coworkers, the people we would not have chosen but who bring us comfort we did not expect. Thank You that Your kingdom has never been built by people who had it all together, only by people who kept showing up. Give us the grace Paul showed Mark: the willingness to publicly restore someone we once publicly rejected. Give us the faithfulness of Aristarchus: the courage to stay when staying costs everything. And give us the quiet steadiness of Jesus Justus: the willingness to serve even when nobody remembers our name. You see every one of us. That is enough. In Jesus' name, Amen.

💬 Discussion Question
What does it cost you to give someone a second chance after a significant failure, and what would the church look like if we practiced that kind of restoration more freely?
