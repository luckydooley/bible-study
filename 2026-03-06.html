📌 Week in Review
Every counterfeit this week pointed to the same gap: something good had been placed where only Christ belongs.
  • ☩Sabbath: The week opened with Colossians 2:16 — one of the most misread verses in the New Testament. Paul was not targeting the fourth commandment; he was targeting the ceremonial calendar being weaponized as a tool of spiritual judgment. The feasts, new moons, and annual sabbaths were shadows. Christ is the substance they always pointed toward.
  • ☩Sunday: All the treasures of wisdom and knowledge are hidden in Christ. The Colossian error was a quiet demotion: adding a hierarchy of intermediaries between the believer and Christ, treating him as the starting point of a spiritual ladder rather than the whole of it.
  • ☩Monday: Walk in Christ the same way you received him. Being rooted and built up in him is not the beginning of the journey; it is the entire journey. There is no supplement, no next level, no advanced initiation available beyond knowing him.
  • ☩Tuesday: The sin-debt was nailed to the Cross. What was cancelled was the record of our charges, the cheirographon, not the moral law that defined them. The debt is gone; the law that exposed it stands.
  • ☩Wednesday: The ceremonial calendar — feasts, new moons, annual sabbaths — were shadows pointing forward to Christ. The substance has arrived. No one who has died with Christ has standing to be judged over the shadows any longer.
  • ☩Thursday: Human commandments layered on top of what Christ finished produce performance, not transformation. Religious rule-keeping has an appearance of wisdom and no actual power over the sinful nature. What looks like spiritual depth is often just pride wearing a hair shirt.
🗺️ Historical & Cultural Context
Colossae: Where Philosophy, Religion, and Anxiety Converged

To understand why Paul's argument in Colossians 2 landed with such force, you have to understand the world Colossae occupied. The city sat in the Lycus Valley in what is now western Turkey, downstream from the larger and more influential cities of Laodicea and Hierapolis. By the time Paul wrote this letter (around AD 60–62, most likely from a Roman prison), Colossae had declined from its former prominence, but the intellectual and religious crosscurrents of the region were if anything more intense for it.

The Lycus Valley was a genuine melting pot. Jewish communities had lived in the region for generations, transplanted there by the Seleucid kings centuries earlier. Those communities had absorbed significant Hellenistic influence along the way: their Judaism was already a hybrid, shaped by Greek philosophical categories and local religious customs. When Christianity arrived, it entered a world already accustomed to synthesis — taking a thread from this tradition and a thread from that one and weaving something that felt comprehensive and intellectually serious.

The specific threat Paul addresses was not crude paganism. It was something far more dangerous: a spiritually serious, intellectually respectable system claiming to offer believers deeper access to God through the right combination of Jewish ceremonial observance, ascetic discipline, mystical vision, and veneration of angelic intermediaries. It felt like more. It felt like advancement. The people promoting it were not obvious frauds; they were the kind of people who read widely, spoke carefully, and made the ordinary believer feel they were missing something. Paul's entire letter is a response to that feeling. His answer: you are missing nothing. The fullness is in Christ. You are in him. You are already complete.

That answer was as countercultural in AD 60 as it is today. Every religious marketplace, ancient or modern, runs on the premise that there is always more to acquire, more rungs to climb, more depths to unlock. Colossians 2 insists the ladder has been removed and replaced with a person. The week we just spent in that chapter is one extended argument for why that is not a reduction but the most radical and liberating claim in the history of religion.

📖 Deeper Questions

A week in Colossians 2 raises questions that don't settle easily — about who Christ is, about what the Cross actually accomplished, about how to hold Scripture when parts of it push back against what feels natural. These four questions are worth taking into the Sabbath.

1
What does it mean that in Christ "dwells all the fullness of the Godhead bodily," and that he "is the head of all principality and power"?
Colossians 2:9–10
☩ Open in Bible App
"For the entire fullness of God's nature dwells bodily in Christ, and you have been filled by him, who is the head over every ruler and authority."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
It means Christ is not a representative of God, a messenger, or the best human approximation. He is God — fully, bodily, without reservation. The Greek word pleroma (fullness) was a technical term in the Colossian false teaching; the opponents believed divine fullness was parceled out across a hierarchy of spiritual beings, each holding a portion. Paul's counter is absolute: the entire fullness is in one place, one Person, one body. There are no beings above Christ to reach. He is the head over all of them, and you have already been filled by him. The question is not how to get more of God. It is whether we actually believe we already have him.
John 1:1
☩ Open in Bible App
"In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
John's prologue grounds Colossians 2:9 in eternity. The Word was not created at the incarnation; he was with God and was God before the first moment of creation. This is not a man elevated to divine status. This is the eternal God who took on human nature. The fullness that dwells in Christ is not something given to him. It is what he has always been.
Hebrews 1:3
☩ Open in Bible App
"The Son is the radiance of God's glory and the exact expression of his nature, sustaining all things by his powerful word. After making purification for sins, he sat down at the right hand of the Majesty on high."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Two images that belong together. Apaugasma (radiance) means the light shining out from its source — not a reflection from outside but the actual outshining. Charakter (exact expression) is a seal-maker's word: the impression that is identical to the seal. Christ does not merely resemble God; he is the precise imprint. And after making purification for sins, he sat down. Old Testament priests never sat in the sanctuary because their work was never finished. The sitting is the declaration that his work is complete — done, settled, sufficient.
1 Peter 3:22
☩ Open in Bible App
"...who has gone into heaven and is at the right hand of God with angels, authorities, and powers subject to him."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Peter closes the circle. Every power in the spiritual universe — the very beings the Colossian false teachers were urging believers to venerate and appease — is subject to the one who finished the work and sat down. This is why the Colossian hierarchy was not just theologically wrong but practically absurd: they were inviting people to seek access to lesser beings when the one to whom all lesser beings bow had already made himself fully available.
2
Colossians 2:14–16 is often used to argue against the seventh-day Sabbath. What other problems come with that argument?
Colossians 2:14–16
☩ Open in Bible App
"He erased the certificate of debt, with its obligations, that was against us and opposed to us, and has taken it away by nailing it to the cross. He disarmed the rulers and authorities and disgraced them publicly; he triumphed over them in him. Therefore, let no one judge you in regard to food and drink or in the matter of a festival or a new moon or a Sabbath day."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
This week established that the sabbaths Paul mentions here are the annual ceremonial festivals, not the seventh-day Sabbath, which predates the entire ceremonial system and was instituted at Creation before sin existed. But beyond that specific argument, the broader interpretation has several problems it cannot solve on its own terms.
Romans 7:7
☩ Open in Bible App
"What should we say then? Is the law sin? Absolutely not! But I would not have known sin if it were not for the law. For example, I would not have known what it is to covet if the law had not said, Do not covet."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Paul defines sin by quoting the tenth commandment directly. If the moral law was abolished at the Cross, sin loses its definition — and if sin has no definition, the Cross has nothing to deal with. You cannot coherently eliminate the law that names the very problem the Cross was meant to solve. The argument defeats itself on Paul's own terms.
Matthew 5:17–18
☩ Open in Bible App
"Don't think that I came to abolish the Law or the Prophets. I did not come to abolish but to fulfill. For truly I tell you, until heaven and earth pass away, not the smallest letter or one stroke of a letter will pass away from the law until all things are accomplished."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Jesus says it plainly. He did not come to abolish the law. His instruction in Matthew 24:20 — to pray that flight not happen on the Sabbath during the future tribulation — assumes Sabbath observance well after the Cross, the resurrection, and Pentecost. And there is a final logical problem: if Colossians 2:16 abolishes the fourth commandment, consistency requires it abolish all ten. No one argues that. The selective application reveals a conclusion driving the interpretation, not the other way around.
3
How do you deal with those who insist that the ceremonial laws must still be kept? What problems arise from that insistence?
1 Corinthians 5:7
☩ Open in Bible App
"For Christ our Passover lamb has been sacrificed."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
The most honest starting point is curiosity before correction. The hunger driving that position — for historical depth, for rootedness, for a sense that something real has been lost — deserves respect before it deserves an argument. But the theological problem is precise: if Passover must still be observed as a requirement, then Christ our Passover did not accomplish what Paul says he did. The shadow is being treated as if the substance has not yet arrived — which means either the Cross was incomplete or Christ is not who Scripture says he is.
Galatians 5:1–3
☩ Open in Bible App
"For freedom, Christ set us free. Stand firm, then, and don't submit again to a yoke of slavery. Take note! I, Paul, am telling you that if you get yourselves circumcised, Christ will not benefit you at all. Again I testify to every man who gets himself circumcised that he is obligated to do the entire law."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Paul's warning here is practical as well as theological: you cannot selectively require portions of the ceremonial system. Accepting one part as an obligation makes a person responsible for all of it — the system was a unified whole. Communities that try to require parts of it tend to produce exactly the judgment culture Colossians 2 warns against: spiritual standing measured by ceremonial compliance, those who observe looking down on those who don't, the whole community oriented around performance rather than toward Christ as the head.
4
Why must we guard against anything that would weaken our faith in the authority and inspiration of all Scripture — even the parts that make us uncomfortable?
2 Timothy 3:16–17
☩ Open in Bible App
"All Scripture is inspired by God and is profitable for teaching, for rebuking, for correcting, for training in righteousness, so that the man of God may be complete, equipped for every good work."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Paul's claim is total: all Scripture. Not the parts that confirm what you already believe, not the parts that feel spiritually elevated — all of it, including the rebuking and the correcting. The word theopneustos (inspired, literally "God-breathed") means the text carries the very breath of God into it. A Bible filtered through skepticism until its hard edges are softened no longer has this power. It stops being a lamp and becomes a mirror — reflecting you back to yourself, unable to show you anything you haven't already decided to see.
2 Peter 1:20–21
☩ Open in Bible App
"Above all, you know this: No prophecy of Scripture comes from the prophet's own interpretation, because no prophecy ever came by the will of man; instead, men spoke from God as they were carried along by the Holy Spirit."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Peter grounds scriptural authority in its origin: it did not come from human will or human wisdom. The prophets were carried along by the Holy Spirit — the image is of a ship driven by wind, the human vessel real and present but the power not their own. This is why the threat rarely arrives as outright rejection. It arrives as sophistication: a growing confidence that some passages are culturally conditioned, that others need reinterpretation in light of modern understanding, that the uncomfortable parts were products of their time. Each step feels reasonable. Each step is small. At the end of enough small steps, the person holding the Bible can no longer tell the difference between what the text actually says and what they have decided it must mean. The guard against it is not defensiveness — it is posture: reading Scripture as the voice of God speaking directly to you, inside a living relationship with the Christ it reveals, willing to be surprised, willing to let the hard parts do their work.
🌍 Still Speaking
The Supplement Industry Never Sleeps

Walk into any bookstore's religion section and you will find the same promise in a hundred different covers: Christianity plus. Christianity plus ancient spiritual disciplines. Christianity plus contemplative mysticism. Christianity plus Jewish roots. Christianity plus neuroscience and attachment theory. Christianity plus the enneagram. Each book begins by identifying something missing — a depth that ordinary believers lack, a richness the tradition has lost — and then offers the tools to acquire it. The authors are often sincere, often scholarly, often pointing toward something genuinely worth recovering. And the hunger they are feeding is real.

But Paul's diagnosis of the Colossian situation fits every one of those books with uncomfortable precision. They have an appearance of wisdom. They promote a kind of self-made spirituality and humility. And they are of no value in actually dealing with what is wrong with us — because the problem is not a deficit of practice or technique or historical knowledge. The problem is sin, and its only solution is a Person. The moment any framework shifts from pointing toward Christ to supplementing him — from being a tributary to the river to presenting itself as a second river — it has become the Colossian heresy in a new cover.

The test Paul offers is not sophistication. It is the question of the head: does this keep you connected to Christ as the source from whom the whole body grows? A practice or tradition that keeps directing you back to him is a gift. One that begins to feel like the point itself — one that generates a sense of spiritual superiority in those who follow it over those who don't — has already started doing what the Colossian teachers did. The ancient text is still speaking directly into the modern religious marketplace, in every aisle of that bookstore.

✝️ Christ at the Center of the Week

Every danger Colossians 2 warned against this week shares a single root: Christ has been moved from the center. Not necessarily denied, not attacked — just quietly shifted. Elevated to first among many rather than Lord over all. Supplemented rather than trusted. Pointed to rather than inhabited. The philosophical system, the ceremonial calendar, the ascetic rules, the angel hierarchy, the human commandments — each one occupied space that belongs only to him.

The remedy Paul offers is not more rigorous theology, though the theology matters. It is a return to the Person. He is the radiance of God's glory and the exact imprint of his nature. He is the Word who was with God and was God before the first moment of creation. He is the one to whom every ruler and authority in the universe is subject — who sat down at the right hand of the Majesty on high, having finished the work. He is the substance the shadows always pointed toward. He is the head the body draws its life from. He is the foundation the debt was cancelled on.

You have been filled by him. Not partially, not provisionally. There is nothing to supplement and no ladder to climb. The fullness is in Christ, available to everyone who simply stays connected to the head.

✅ Weekend Practice

Just to hear it as the voice of God speaking directly to you. Then sit with it for five minutes without reaching for your phone. Let it do what it is trying to do.
