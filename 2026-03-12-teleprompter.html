What's running in the background of your mind right now?

Not what you're thinking about on purpose — I mean what's just... looping. The thing that's there when nothing else is competing for your attention. For most of us, if we're honest, it's some variation of anxiety. Or resentment. Or comparison. Just playing on shuffle all day long.

Here's the passage. Colossians 3:15 through 17 from the Christian Standard Bible.

Read verbatim · CSB

"And let the peace of Christ, to which you were also called in one body, rule your hearts. And be thankful. Let the word of Christ dwell richly among you, in all wisdom teaching and admonishing one another through psalms, hymns, and spiritual songs, singing to God with gratitude in your hearts. And whatever you do, in word or in deed, do everything in the name of the Lord Jesus, giving thanks to God the Father through him."

Now — one thing you need to know to hear this correctly.

Paul is writing to people living inside the Roman Empire. And every single person in that world knew the phrase pax Romana — the peace of Rome. It was everywhere. On coins, in speeches, carved into monuments. Rome had brought peace to the world.

Except everyone also knew what that peace actually looked like. It was enforced by legions. It was maintained by crucifixion. The pax Romana was peace imposed from the outside. Obey, and you have peace. Cross us, and the peace disappears.

Paul introduces something completely different. The pax Christi. The peace of Christ. And this peace doesn't come from outside at all. It operates from within. It rules the heart. And for people living under Rome, that was not just a religious idea — it was a genuinely revolutionary one.

03 The Deep Dive On Cam 2:15–6:15

So let's get into what Paul is actually saying. Three pieces to this.

The first one is about peace. Verse 15: "Let the peace of Christ rule your hearts."

That word "rule" — in the Greek it's brabeueto. And a brabeutes was the umpire at the Greek athletic games. The person who made the final call. Settled disputes. Awarded the prize. Paul is saying: let the peace of Christ be the umpire inside you.

Think about what that actually means. When anxiety and trust are both competing for your attention. When resentment and forgiveness are both making their case. When you're about to make a decision and you're not sure which way to go. Paul is saying there's an authority inside you — if you'll let it function — that can settle that dispute. Not by making you feel calm, but by actually making the call. Peace as decision-maker. Not just a mood. An umpire.

And he adds one phrase that I think we rush past: "to which you were also called in one body." The peace of Christ isn't just personal. It's communal. You were called into this peace together. Which means when there's conflict in the church — when two people can't agree, when resentment starts building between members — the peace of Christ is supposed to function there too. Same umpire. Same authority. Available to the whole body, not just to individuals.

· · ·

The second piece is about the word of Christ. Verse 16: "Let the word of Christ dwell richly among you."

That word enoikeito — dwell — means to take up residence. Move in. Make a home. This is not "read your Bible occasionally." This is Scripture settling into every room. And the word plousios, richly, means lavishly, without scarcity. Paul is saying: don't ration it. Let it fill the place.

And then he says the word dwells richly through "teaching and admonishing one another through psalms, hymns, and spiritual songs." This is the part that gets interesting. Music is not decoration in Paul's vision of the church. Music is a vehicle for theology. It is one of the primary means by which the word of Christ actually dwells in a community. The songs you sing are teaching you. The hymns are shaping your theology. And you're doing that for each other — not performing, not being performed at. Mutually instructing one another through the act of worship.

This is why what you sing matters. And it's why David's harp could bring relief to Saul's tormented spirit in 1 Samuel 16 — and why the same harp could provoke Saul to violence in 1 Samuel 18. The music is powerful. But the condition of the heart determines whether it brings healing or makes things worse. Paul specifies: "singing to God with gratitude in your hearts." The heart's posture matters as much as the song.

· · ·

The third piece is the capstone. Verse 17: "Whatever you do, in word or in deed, do everything in the name of the Lord Jesus."

Leave no category untouched. That's what Paul is going for here. It's not "do your spiritual activities in Jesus' name." It's whatever. Word or deed. Every conversation. Every action. Every decision. The filter is: can this be done in Jesus' name? Does it represent him? Is it consistent with his character? If the answer is no, it doesn't belong in the new life.

And here's what I want you to notice — and this is the Christ Connection that ties the whole thing together. Look whose name is on everything in this passage. The peace of Christ. The word of Christ. The name of the Lord Jesus. This is not a self-help program with spiritual vocabulary. Every element of this operating system is anchored in a Person.

The peace that umpires your heart — Jesus spoke that same peace over a storm on Galilee. He breathed it on his disciples after the resurrection. "Peace be with you." The word that dwells richly — that's not abstract wisdom. The Word became flesh and dwelt among us. John 1:14. And doing everything "in the name of the Lord Jesus" is only possible because Jesus earned that name. He humbled himself to death on a cross, and God exalted him and gave him the name above every name. Philippians 2:9.

The new life isn't about becoming a better person through spiritual discipline. It's about living inside the reality of who Jesus is and what he's already done.

04 Today's World B-Roll 6:15–8:15
🎥 Drone over water, tree canopy, or open sky. Or go on-camera if you prefer the personal feel here.

In June 2025, researchers at the University of Melbourne published the largest meta-analysis ever conducted on music therapy and anxiety. The study appeared in eClinicalMedicine, a Lancet journal. They analyzed data from clinical trials spanning multiple decades and found that music therapy produced significant reductions in anxiety across mental health, medical, and workplace settings.

The most effective approaches were receptive — intentional listening — and combined formats that included active participation. The researchers noted that the anxiety-reducing effects were comparable to first-line psychotherapies and even some pharmacological treatments.

A separate 2025 scoping review in JMIR Mental Health confirmed that music interventions reduced cortisol levels — that's the body's primary stress hormone — and improved emotional regulation across diverse populations.

Paul told the Colossians to let the word of Christ dwell richly through psalms, hymns, and spiritual songs. He was not prescribing background noise. He was describing a practice that modern neuroscience now confirms reshapes the brain's stress response and creates the internal conditions where peace can actually rule.

David's harp soothed Saul's tormented spirit three thousand years ago. The mechanism, it turns out, is not mysterious. Music changes the body. When the music carries truth, it changes the soul.

Paul knew this before the researchers did.

05 The Takeaway On Cam 8:15–9:15

Here's the one thing I want you to do today.

Replace one thing in your daily routine. If your morning starts with news or social media — try starting with a psalm or a worship song instead. If your commute defaults to talk radio or podcasts, switch to music that carries truth. Whatever the equivalent is for you — find one slot and swap it.

Paul says the word of Christ is meant to dwell richly in you. That means giving it real estate in your actual day. Not just Sunday morning. Not just when you're already in a good mood.

Pay attention to how the change affects your internal state. Notice what the background playlist does to what comes out of you when pressure hits.

Whatever you do, in word or deed — do it in Jesus' name. Start by letting Jesus' word be the thing running in the background.
