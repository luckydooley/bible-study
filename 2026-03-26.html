Thursday, A Message for Laodicea

Colossians · Week 13, Day 6

✦ ☩ ✦
📌 TL;DR

Paul's letter was not meant to stay in Colossae. It was designed to circulate, and the church it was headed to next had no idea how badly it would need the warning decades later.

  • ☩ Paul instructs the Colossians to share their letter with Laodicea and to read a letter coming from Laodicea in return, revealing that apostolic letters were not private correspondence but shared resources for the entire network of churches.
  • ☩ Archippus receives a pointed personal charge to fulfill the ministry he received in the Lord, a public accountability statement tucked into the closing of a letter.
  • ☩ Paul closes with his own handwritten signature, a brief plea to remember his chains, and a benediction of grace, compressing an entire theology of suffering and hope into three sentences.
  • ☩ The Laodicean connection becomes haunting when read alongside Revelation 3:14-21, where Jesus later addresses this same church as lukewarm, self-satisfied, and blind to its own poverty.
🔁 Connection to Yesterday

Yesterday we watched the contrast between Luke and Demas, between staying and leaving, between loving the kingdom and loving the present world. Today Paul closes his letter with final instructions that pull the camera back one last time: this letter was never meant for Colossae alone. It was meant for Laodicea too. And when we read what Jesus later said to the Laodicean church in Revelation, the urgency of Paul's instruction to share the letter takes on a weight he may not have fully anticipated.

🗺️ Historical & Cultural Context
The Wealthiest Church in the Valley Got the Hardest Letter

Laodicea was the banking capital of the Lycus Valley, famous enough for its wealth that when an earthquake devastated the region in AD 60, Laodicea declined Roman disaster relief and rebuilt itself with its own money. The Roman historian Tacitus recorded this with something like admiration. The city was also known for a medical school that produced a renowned eye salve and for a thriving textile industry specializing in glossy black wool. These three industries, banking, medicine, and clothing, would become the exact three metaphors Jesus used in His rebuke of the Laodicean church in Revelation 3: "I advise you to buy from me gold refined in the fire... white clothes... and ointment to spread on your eyes."

When Paul wrote Colossians around AD 60-62, Laodicea was a sister church in the same region, close enough to share letters and greet each other's members. But by the time John recorded Christ's message to Laodicea in Revelation (likely around AD 95), the church had become the poster child for comfortable, self-deceived Christianity. The letter Paul told the Colossians to share with Laodicea was, in retrospect, a prophylactic: the theology of Christ's supremacy, the warnings against empty philosophy, the call to set your minds on things above. Laodicea needed every word of it. Whether they listened is another question entirely.

📖 Into the Word

Today's anchor passage is Colossians 4:16-18, Paul's final instructions and sign-off. The supporting passage from Revelation 3:15-20 shows what happened to the Laodicean church decades later, and Colossians 2:1-3 reminds us that Paul had been fighting for this church's soul from the very beginning of the letter.

Colossians 4:16-18
☩ Open in Bible App
"After this letter has been read at your gathering, have it read also in the church of the Laodiceans; and see that you also read the letter from Laodicea. And tell Archippus, 'Pay attention to the ministry you have received in the Lord, so that you can accomplish it.' I, Paul, am writing this greeting with my own hand. Remember my chains. Grace be with you."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Three remarkable things happen in these closing verses. First, Paul commands a letter exchange. The Colossian letter must go to Laodicea, and a letter "from Laodicea" (likely Paul's letter to the Ephesians, or a now-lost letter that circulated through the region) must come to Colossae. This reveals that Paul's letters were not private pastoral notes; they were public documents meant to circulate among churches. The theology Paul developed for one community was meant to strengthen others facing similar pressures. Second, Archippus receives a public charge: "Pay attention to the ministry you have received in the Lord, so that you can accomplish it." The Greek blepe ("pay attention, watch out") carries urgency, almost a warning. Archippus is also mentioned in Philemon 2 as a "fellow soldier," suggesting he held a leadership role, possibly in Laodicea. Paul's public charge is a form of communal accountability: the entire church network now knows what Archippus was called to do. Third, Paul picks up the pen himself. The rest of the letter was dictated to a scribe, but the final greeting is in Paul's own handwriting, authenticating the document. Then three final words, each one loaded. "Remember my chains": a reminder that the theology in this letter was forged under persecution, not in a comfortable study. "Grace be with you": the same word that opened the letter (Colossians 1:2) now closes it. Everything between those two graces is framed by the unmerited favor of God.
Revelation 3:15-18, 20
☩ Open in Bible App
"I know your works, that you are neither cold nor hot. I wish that you were cold or hot. So, because you are lukewarm, and neither hot nor cold, I am going to vomit you out of my mouth. For you say, 'I'm rich; I have become wealthy and need nothing,' and you don't realize that you are wretched, pitiful, poor, blind, and naked. I advise you to buy from me gold refined in the fire so that you may be rich, white clothes so that you may be dressed and your shameful nakedness not be exposed, and ointment to spread on your eyes so that you may see. ... See! I stand at the door and knock. If anyone hears my voice and opens the door, I will come in to him and eat with him, and he with me."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Decades after Paul told the Colossians to share their letter with Laodicea, Jesus Himself addresses this church through John, and the diagnosis is devastating. The Laodiceans had become exactly what the Colossian letter warned against: self-sufficient, philosophically comfortable, and convinced they needed nothing. Jesus uses their own city's proudest products as weapons: you think you are rich, but you need gold from Me; you think you are clothed, but you are naked; you think you can see, but you are blind. The Laodicean water supply, piped from hot springs through miles of aqueduct, arrived lukewarm and mineral-heavy, famously unpleasant to drink. Jesus uses this local reality as His central metaphor. But even this severe rebuke ends with one of the most tender images in all of Scripture: Christ standing at the door, knocking, waiting to be invited in. The Judge who could force entry chooses instead to knock. The church that needed to hear Colossians most urgently is the same church that would later need to hear Christ's knock most desperately.
Colossians 2:1-3
☩ Open in Bible App
"For I want you to know how greatly I am struggling for you, for those in Laodicea, and for all who have not seen me in person. I want their hearts to be encouraged and joined together in love, so that they may have all the riches of complete understanding and have the knowledge of God's mystery, Christ. In him are hidden all the treasures of wisdom and knowledge."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Paul named Laodicea at the very beginning of this letter, not just at the end. His struggle, his agonizing prayer, his theological argument about Christ's supremacy over all philosophies and powers was always intended for both communities. The "riches of complete understanding" Paul wanted for them is the exact opposite of the false riches the Laodiceans would later claim to possess. Paul was fighting for their souls before they even knew they were in danger. The Colossian letter, circulated as Paul instructed, was his preemptive strike against the spiritual complacency that would eventually define Laodicea.
🎯 Street Level
The Doctor's Report You Did Not Want to Open

You know the feeling. The lab results are back. The envelope sits on your counter. Part of you wants to open it immediately. Part of you wants to leave it sealed because as long as you do not read it, you do not have to deal with what it says. Laodicea left the envelope sealed. They had Paul's letter circulating in their network. They had Epaphras praying for them from Rome. They had every resource they needed to stay spiritually healthy. And somewhere along the way, they convinced themselves they were fine. "I'm rich. I have become wealthy. I need nothing." The diagnosis was already in the envelope; they just stopped reading. That is what spiritual complacency does. It does not argue with the truth. It simply stops paying attention to it. The letter is on the counter. The question is whether you will open it.

🌍 Today's World, Ancient Truth
The Self-Assessment Gap in American Christianity

A 2022 Barna study found that 42% of American pastors had seriously considered quitting ministry in the previous year. But buried in the same data set was a more subtle finding: among churches where significant conflict existed, a substantial number of congregants reported being unaware of any problems at all. The gap between how church leadership experienced the church and how the average attendee experienced it was, in many cases, enormous. Leaders saw stress fractures. Congregants saw a building that looked fine from the outside.

This is the Laodicean condition in modern dress. The most dangerous spiritual state is not open rebellion against God; it is the quiet conviction that everything is fine when it is not. Laodicea did not reject Christ. They simply stopped noticing that He was outside, knocking. The wealthier the church, the easier it was to mistake financial stability for spiritual health. Paul's instruction to share letters between churches was, in part, an antidote to exactly this kind of isolation. When you only hear your own community's voice, you lose the ability to diagnose your own condition. You need an outside perspective. You need Paul's letter from Rome, or Epaphras's prayers from prison, or a friend who loves you enough to tell you what you cannot see.

✝️ The Christ Connection

The entire letter of Colossians has been building toward one reality: Christ is supreme over everything, and everything finds its meaning in Him. Paul's closing instruction to share this letter with Laodicea was an act of love aimed at a church that would one day forget this truth. And when they did forget, Christ did not abandon them. He stood at their door and knocked. The same Jesus who holds all things together (Colossians 1:17), who is the head of the body (Colossians 1:18), who is the mystery in whom all treasures of wisdom are hidden (Colossians 2:3), did not kick down the door of a lukewarm church. He knocked. He waited. He offered to come in and eat with them, the most intimate act of fellowship in the ancient world. That is the Christ this entire quarter has been revealing: not a distant theological concept but a Person who pursues, who waits, and who never stops offering Himself to people who have forgotten how much they need Him.

✅ Personal Application Challenge
Ask someone you trust, a spouse, a close friend, a mentor, this question today: "Is there anything in my spiritual life that looks lukewarm from the outside that I might not be seeing from the inside?" Give them permission to be honest. Do not defend yourself. Just listen. Laodicea's problem was not a lack of resources; it was a lack of outside perspective. Let someone who loves you be your mirror today.
🙏 Closing Prayer

Lord Jesus, we hear You knocking. We have spent this quarter in Your letter to the Colossians, learning who You are: the image of the invisible God, the firstborn over all creation, the one in whom all things hold together. And now, at the end, we find You standing at a door, asking to be let in. Forgive us for the times we have been Laodicea, convinced we had everything we needed while You stood outside. Open our eyes to see our real condition. Give us the courage to invite outside voices into our lives, to read the letters we have been avoiding, to hear the diagnoses we have been delaying. And thank You, Lord, that even when we are lukewarm, You are not. Your love burns. Your knock persists. Your grace, the first word of this letter and the last, never stops reaching for us. In Jesus' name, Amen.
