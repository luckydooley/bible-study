Colossians · Week 12, Day 4

✦ ☩ ✦
📌 TL;DR

Paul transforms the most exploitative relationship in the ancient world by telling slaves they serve Christ, and telling masters they answer to one.

  • ☩ Paul addresses slaves as moral agents with real dignity, giving them the longest instruction in his household code and promising them an inheritance from the Lord.
  • ☩ The revolutionary principle is audience: "Whatever you do, do it from the heart, as something done for the Lord and not for people." Your real employer is Christ.
  • ☩ Masters receive a single, devastating verse: deal justly and fairly, because you too have a Master in heaven. God is watching from the other side of the desk.
  • ☩ While Paul does not call for the abolition of slavery in this letter, he plants seeds that make the institution incompatible with the gospel over time.
🔁 Connection to Yesterday

Yesterday Paul addressed parents and children: obey in everything, but do not crush the spirit. Today he moves to the third and final ring of the Roman household: the relationship between masters and slaves. The pattern holds. Paul addresses the weaker party first with a command that dignifies them, then turns to the stronger party with an obligation that limits them. But today's passage goes further than either of the previous two. Paul gives slaves the longest section in the entire household code, the most theological motivation, and an astonishing promise: you will receive the reward of an inheritance from the Lord. In a world where slaves could not legally inherit anything, Paul says God has already written their name in the will.

🗺️ Historical & Cultural Context
Slavery in the Roman Empire Was Not What You Think

An estimated one-third of the population of the Roman Empire consisted of slaves. But Roman slavery was not identical to the chattel slavery of the American South. Roman slaves could include educated teachers, skilled physicians, household managers, and artisans. Some earned wages, saved money, and purchased their freedom. Others were born into slavery, captured in war, or sold to pay debts. The legal status was uniform, a slave was property, but the lived experience varied enormously. A slave tutoring a senator's children in Greek literature lived a very different life than a slave working in a mine.

Paul's letter would have been read aloud to the entire Colossian congregation, which almost certainly included both slaves and masters sitting in the same room. When Paul addresses slaves directly, speaking to them as people with choices, motivations, and a relationship with Christ, he is doing something no Roman gathering would have done. He is treating them as full participants in the community of faith. And when he tells masters that they have a Master in heaven, he is leveling a hierarchy that Roman law had built to be permanent. Paul is not writing a political manifesto. He is planting theological dynamite that will take centuries to detonate.

📖 Into the Word

Today's anchor passage is Colossians 3:22-25 and 4:1, Paul's instructions to slaves and masters. This is the longest section in the household code, which itself is significant: Paul devotes the most theological energy to the relationship with the greatest power imbalance. The supporting passages from the Old Testament show that God had already placed limits on the institution of slavery long before Paul wrote, and the letter to Philemon shows Paul applying these principles to a real person.

Colossians 3:22
☩ Open in Bible App
"Slaves, obey your human masters in everything. Don't work only while being watched, as people-pleasers, but work wholeheartedly, fearing the Lord."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Paul begins with the same comprehensive command he gave children: obey "in everything." But then he immediately reframes the motivation. The contrast is between ophthalmodoulia (eye-service, working only when the boss is watching) and haploteti kardias (sincerity of heart, literally "singleness of heart"). A person-pleaser adjusts their effort based on who is in the room. A Christ-follower works the same way whether anyone is looking or not, because the audience that matters is always watching. The phrase "fearing the Lord" is the key: the slave's ultimate accountability is not to the human master but to God. This does not make the work easier, but it makes it sacred.
Colossians 3:23-24
☩ Open in Bible App
"Whatever you do, do it from the heart, as something done for the Lord and not for people, knowing that you will receive the reward of an inheritance from the Lord. You serve the Lord Christ."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
This is the theological heart of the passage and one of the most universally applicable verses Paul ever wrote. The phrase "from the heart" translates ek psyches (from the soul). Paul is saying: pour your whole self into whatever task is in front of you, not because the task deserves it, but because you are really serving Christ through it. Then comes the promise that would have electrified every slave in the room: "you will receive the reward of an inheritance." Under Roman law, slaves could not inherit. They were property, not heirs. Paul declares that God has overruled Roman law. The inheritance is not from the master; it is "from the Lord." The final sentence is both a comfort and a commission: "You serve the Lord Christ." Your real employer has nail scars in his hands.
Colossians 3:25
☩ Open in Bible App
"For the wrongdoer will be paid back for whatever wrong he has done, and there is no favoritism."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
This verse cuts in both directions. For slaves tempted to steal, sabotage, or slack off, it is a warning: God sees. But for slaves suffering under unjust masters, it is a promise: God sees that too. The phrase "there is no favoritism" (literally "no receiving of faces") means God does not adjust His justice based on social status. The master who exploits a slave will face the same impartial Judge as the slave who cheats a master. In a society built entirely on favoritism, where your legal rights depended on your rank, this was a seismic claim.
Colossians 4:1
☩ Open in Bible App
"Masters, deal with your slaves justly and fairly, since you know that you too have a Master in heaven."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
One verse. That is all the masters get. But what a verse. The Greek words dikaion (just) and isoteta (fairness, equality) would have been uncomfortable for any slave owner to hear. Paul is not saying masters and slaves are socially equal in the Roman system. He is saying that before God, the playing field is level. And the reason is devastating: "you too have a Master in heaven." Every master is also a slave, accountable to a Lord who sees everything and plays no favorites. The brief letter to Philemon shows what this looks like in practice: Paul sends the runaway slave Onesimus back and asks Philemon to receive him "no longer as a slave, but more than a slave, as a dearly loved brother."
1 Peter 2:16
☩ Open in Bible App
"Submit as free people, not using your freedom as a cover-up for evil, but as God's slaves."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Peter captures the paradox at the heart of Christian work ethics: true freedom is not the absence of authority but the choice of whom you serve. Every believer, regardless of their social position, is simultaneously free in Christ and voluntarily bound to God's service. Freedom is not a license to do whatever you want; it is the power to do what is right, even when no one is making you.
Deuteronomy 15:12
☩ Open in Bible App
"If your fellow Hebrew, a man or woman, is sold to you and serves you six years, you must set him free in the seventh year."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Long before Paul, the Mosaic law placed limits on the institution of servitude that had no parallel in the ancient world. Israelites could not hold fellow Israelites as permanent slaves. The six-year limit, combined with laws requiring fair treatment (Leviticus 25:39-43), made Israelite servitude fundamentally different from the surrounding cultures. God was already moving the moral arc toward freedom, one law at a time.
🎯 Street Level
The Security Camera You Cannot Unplug

Most workplaces have security cameras. Some employees adjust their behavior based on whether the camera is running: they work harder when they think someone is watching and ease up when the coast is clear. Paul calls this "eye-service," and he says it is the opposite of how a Christian should work. His alternative is not to install a better camera. It is to realize that you already work for someone who sees everything: not as a surveillance system waiting to catch you slacking, but as a Lord who notices every effort, values every act of integrity, and is building an inheritance for you based on what He sees. You are not working for a paycheck, a performance review, or a promotion. You are working for a Person who already gave everything He had for you. That changes the Monday morning feeling entirely.

🌍 Today's World, Ancient Truth
The Global Engagement Crisis

Gallup's State of the Global Workplace 2025 report, surveying nearly a quarter-million workers across 160 countries, found that global employee engagement fell to just 21% in 2024, the lowest level since the pandemic. Only one in five workers worldwide is genuinely engaged in their work. The rest are either "quietly quitting" (doing the minimum required) or actively disengaged (undermining their organizations). Gallup estimates the cost of this disengagement at $438 billion in lost productivity annually.

The research identified a striking pattern: 70% of the variance in team engagement is attributable to the manager. When managers are disengaged, their teams follow. Manager engagement itself dropped from 30% to 27% in a single year, with young managers and female managers experiencing the steepest declines. Gallup found that less than half of all managers globally had received any formal training in management.

Paul was writing to a world where most workers had no choice about their employment, no path to promotion, and no HR department to file a complaint with. Yet his instruction was not "endure it until something better comes along." It was: "whatever you do, do it from the heart, as something done for the Lord." And to those in charge, his instruction was equally clear: deal justly and fairly, because your Master is watching. The modern workplace desperately needs both messages: workers who bring their whole selves to the task because they serve Christ, and leaders who treat people justly because they answer to one.

✝️ The Christ Connection

The sentence "You serve the Lord Christ" in verse 24 is the hinge of the entire passage. Paul takes the most degrading relationship in the ancient world and turns it into a portrait of the gospel. Christ himself "took the form of a servant" (Philippians 2:7). He washed feet. He served tables. He did the work nobody wanted to do, and He did it from the heart, for love. When a Christian worker, whether in a first-century household or a twenty-first-century cubicle, brings their whole soul to a task that nobody notices, they are imitating the One who knelt with a towel and a basin and said, "I have given you an example" (John 13:15).

✅ Personal Application Challenge
Identify one task you regularly do at work, at home, or in your community that feels invisible, unappreciated, or beneath you. The next time you do it, consciously say to yourself before you begin: "I am doing this for the Lord Christ." Notice whether it changes how you approach the task.
🙏 Closing Prayer

Lord Jesus, you took the form of a servant and showed us that no honest work is beneath the One who made the universe. Forgive us for the eye-service, the half-hearted effort we give when we think no one is watching. Forgive us for treating people under our authority as less than the image-bearers you created them to be. Teach us to work from the soul, not for applause or a paycheck, but because we serve you. And for those trapped in work that feels meaningless, exploitative, or crushing, remind them today that you see every effort, you value every act of faithfulness, and you are building an inheritance that no employer can give or take away. In Jesus' name, Amen.
