Tuesday, Standing Perfect and Complete

Colossians · Week 13, Day 4

✦ ☩ ✦
📌 TL;DR

Epaphras did not just pray for the Colossians. He wrestled for them, agonizing in prayer the way an athlete strains for the finish line.

  • ☩ Epaphras, the man who founded the Colossian church, is now in Rome with Paul, and his primary occupation is not preaching or strategizing but praying with agonizing intensity for the churches he planted.
  • ☩ Paul breaks down the content of Epaphras's prayer into four components: that the Colossians would stand firm, mature, fully assured, and rooted in everything God wills.
  • ☩ Epaphras's prayer coverage extended beyond Colossae to the neighboring cities of Laodicea and Hierapolis, revealing a pastor whose concern did not stop at his own congregation's borders.
  • ☩ The passage redefines what "purpose-driven" looks like: not a program, not a strategy, but a clear focus on standing complete in God's will, sustained by relentless prayer.
🔁 Connection to Yesterday

Yesterday we met Paul's three Jewish coworkers: Aristarchus the fellow prisoner, Mark the restored deserter, and Jesus Justus the quietly faithful. Today we shift from the Jewish members of Paul's team to the Gentile members, beginning with the most important Gentile in the entire Colossian story: Epaphras, the man who brought the gospel to Colossae in the first place. If yesterday was about who stands beside you in the work, today is about who stands behind you in prayer.

🗺️ Historical & Cultural Context
Three Cities, One Pastor, Zero Proximity

Colossae, Laodicea, and Hierapolis sat within a 10-mile triangle in the Lycus Valley of present-day Turkey. All three cities likely received the gospel through the same man: Epaphras, described in Colossians 1:7 as a "faithful minister of Christ on your behalf." Paul had never visited these churches personally. Everything he knew about them, he knew through Epaphras. Now Epaphras was in Rome, possibly voluntarily imprisoned alongside Paul (Philemon 23 calls him a "fellow prisoner"), and his churches were over a thousand miles away and under philosophical pressure from false teachers.

In that situation, most leaders would strategize. They would draft action plans, write position papers, or send urgent instructions. Epaphras did something the ancient world would have considered either profoundly spiritual or profoundly impractical: he prayed. Not casually, not briefly, but with the intensity of a wrestler in a match. Paul's description of Epaphras's prayer life uses the Greek word for athletic struggle. This was not a man adding his churches to a prayer list. This was a man fighting for them on his knees, from a prison cell, across an empire.

📖 Into the Word

Today's anchor passage is Colossians 4:12-13, where Paul gives us the most detailed portrait of Epaphras in the entire New Testament. The supporting passage from Colossians 1:9-10 shows that Epaphras's prayer echoes Paul's own prayer for the Colossians from the opening of the letter, revealing a shared pastoral vision that shaped the entire epistle.

Colossians 4:12-13
☩ Open in Bible App
"Epaphras, who is one of you, a servant of Christ Jesus, sends you greetings. He is always wrestling for you in his prayers, so that you can stand mature and fully assured in everything God wills. For I testify about him that he works hard for you, for those in Laodicea, and for those in Hierapolis."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Paul packs an extraordinary amount into these two verses. First, Epaphras is identified as "one of you," a Colossian native, and as a doulos (slave/servant) of Christ Jesus, the same title Paul claims for himself in Romans 1:1. Then comes the key verb: "wrestling" (agonizomai), from which we get the English word "agonize." This is the language of athletic competition, of a wrestler locked in a match, straining every muscle. Paul uses the same word in Colossians 1:29 to describe his own ministry effort. Epaphras is not whispering bedtime prayers; he is fighting a spiritual battle on behalf of people he cannot reach any other way. The content of his prayer contains four interconnected goals. First, "stand" (stathete): to hold firm, unmoved, like a soldier who refuses to break ranks. Second, "mature" (teleioi): the perfection of character that finds its ultimate expression in sacrificial love, as in Matthew 5:48. Third, "fully assured" (plerophoremenoi): completely convinced, carrying the full weight of certainty in one's faith. And fourth, "in everything God wills": comprehensive obedience, not selective compliance. Paul then personally testifies (marturo, a legal witness term) that Epaphras "works hard," using a word that other manuscripts render as "has great zeal." His concern spans all three Lycus Valley cities. This is a pastor who refuses to limit his prayer coverage to his own congregation.
Colossians 1:9-10
☩ Open in Bible App
"For this reason also, since the day we heard this, we haven't stopped praying for you. We are asking that you may be filled with the knowledge of his will in all wisdom and spiritual understanding, so that you may walk worthy of the Lord, fully pleasing to him: bearing fruit in every good work and growing in the knowledge of God."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Compare this with Epaphras's prayer in 4:12. Paul prays for the Colossians to be "filled with the knowledge of his will." Epaphras prays for them to "stand fully assured in everything God wills." Paul prays for them to "walk worthy of the Lord." Epaphras prays for them to "stand mature." The vocabulary is different, but the vision is identical: a community so saturated with the knowledge of God's will that their entire lives become an expression of it. This is not a coincidence. Epaphras and Paul had clearly spent significant time together in Rome, shaping each other's prayers, aligning their pastoral vision, and building the theological framework that became this letter. Epaphras's prayer was not an afterthought to the letter; it was the engine behind it.
🎯 Street Level
The Spotter Who Never Leaves the Gym

If you have ever lifted weights seriously, you know the difference between having a spotter and not having one. A good spotter does not lift the weight for you. They stand behind you, hands hovering, watching your form, ready to catch the bar if you fail. Their presence changes what you are willing to attempt. You load more weight. You push for one more rep. You go to failure because you know someone is there to keep the bar from crushing you. Epaphras was the Colossians' spotter, except he was doing it from 1,200 miles away, and his method was prayer. He could not visit them. He could not preach to them. He could not correct their theology in person. All he could do was agonize before God on their behalf, asking that they would be strong enough to hold the weight of faithfulness in a culture that was pressing down hard. That is what intercessory prayer looks like when it is real: not a polite mention, but a relentless, sweat-soaked refusal to let someone you love collapse under the weight they are carrying.

🌍 Today's World, Ancient Truth
One Billion Prayers from a Phone Screen

In June 2025, the Catholic prayer app Hallow announced that its users had collectively prayed over one billion prayers since the app launched in December 2018. The platform, which has been downloaded nearly 24 million times across 150 countries, grew from a startup founded by three former consultants into the most downloaded religious app in the world. During Lent 2025 alone, the app saw nearly 500,000 downloads in a single day.

The numbers are staggering, but here is what makes them relevant to Colossians 4:12: Hallow's growth was fueled not primarily by content or celebrity endorsements but by community prayer features that allowed users to pray for each other across distances. Shared prayer campaigns, where users committed to pray for specific intentions alongside thousands of strangers, became the app's most powerful retention tool. People came for the guided meditations. They stayed because they discovered that knowing someone else was praying for them, even someone they had never met, fundamentally changed how they experienced their faith.

Epaphras would have understood this immediately. He was doing exactly what Hallow's users are discovering in 2025: praying with agonizing sincerity for people he could not see, trusting that the God who connected them was bigger than the miles between them. The technology is new. The instinct is as old as the church itself.

✝️ The Christ Connection

Epaphras's wrestling prayer is a shadow of something greater. The writer of Hebrews tells us that Jesus Himself "always lives to intercede" for His people (Hebrews 7:25). What Epaphras did from a Roman prison, Christ does eternally from the right hand of the Father. And the content of their prayers aligns: Epaphras wanted the Colossians to stand mature and fully assured in God's will. Jesus prays that His followers would be sanctified in truth (John 17:17) and kept from the evil one (John 17:15). Epaphras's agonizing prayer was not just pastoral concern; it was participation in the very ministry of Christ. Every time we intercede for someone, we join a prayer that Jesus has been praying since before we opened our mouths.

✅ Personal Application Challenge
Choose one person in your life who is carrying a heavy load right now. Today, pray for them with Epaphras-level specificity: not "bless them," but that they would stand firm under the pressure, grow in maturity through the trial, become fully convinced of God's faithfulness, and align their choices with God's will. Pray those four things by name. Then tell the person you prayed for them. Do not say what you prayed. Just let them know they are not carrying the weight alone.
🙏 Closing Prayer

Father, we confess that our prayers are often thin where they should be thick. We mention people when we should be wrestling for them. We offer You vague requests when You invite us to bring specific, agonizing, detailed pleas on behalf of the people we love. Teach us to pray the way Epaphras prayed: with the intensity of a wrestler, the specificity of a pastor, and the reach of a heart that refuses to stop at the borders of convenience. Thank You that while we are still learning to intercede, Your Son never stops. He is always living to pray for us. Let our small prayers join His great one. In Jesus' name, Amen.
