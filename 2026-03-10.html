Tuesday, Renewed in Knowledge

Colossians · Week 11, Day 4

✦ ☩ ✦
📌 TL;DR

Conversion isn't a feeling — it's a complete change of wardrobe, from the inside out.

  • ☩ The "but now" of verse 8 marks a decisive line: the old life of anger, slander, and deception belongs to who we were, not who we are.
  • ☩ The old self is not just reformed — it is stripped off and replaced, like filthy clothes exchanged for clean ones.
  • ☩ The new self is being continuously renewed in knowledge shaped by Christ's own image, not our habits or culture.
  • ☩ This transformation obliterates every social, ethnic, and cultural division — in Christ, those categories no longer define us.
🗺️ Historical & Cultural Context
Colossae Was a City of Categories

The ancient city of Colossae sat at a crossroads — literally. Situated in the Lycus Valley of modern-day Turkey, it was a meeting point of Greek, Phrygian, Jewish, and Roman populations. Every one of those groups carried fierce loyalty to their own ethnic and cultural identity. "Greek and Jew, circumcision and uncircumcision, barbarian, Scythian, slave and free" — these weren't just abstract theological categories. They were the lived reality of daily life in a city like Colossae. Barbarians were considered uncivilized by Greeks. Scythians were the most feared and despised of all — nomadic warriors from the northern steppes, viewed as barely human by the Mediterranean world. Slaves had no legal personhood. The social lines were hard, and crossing them was nearly unthinkable.

Into that world, Paul declares that the new creation in Christ recognizes none of those walls. This wasn't merely a warm sentiment — it was a social earthquake. When Paul writes that the new self renders all those categories obsolete, he is describing a community that would have looked genuinely strange and subversive to anyone watching from the outside.

📖 Into the Word

Today's anchor passage is Colossians 3:6–11, where Paul pivots from sins of desire (yesterday's study) to sins of speech and social fracture. The central question is this: what does genuine transformation look like from the inside — not just restrained behavior, but a completely new self? To answer that, Paul reaches for the "old man / new man" image, a clothing metaphor that runs through his letters and carries enormous theological weight. The supporting passages from Romans, James, and Ephesians illuminate different facets of the same reality: that the old self was crucified with Christ, that we are accountable for how we speak, and that this new creation is being continuously reshaped into the image of God himself.

Colossians 3:6–11
☩ Open in Bible App
"Because of these, God's wrath is coming on the disobedient, and you once walked in these things when you were living in them. But now, put away all the following: anger, wrath, malice, slander, and filthy language from your mouth. Do not lie to one another, since you have put off the old self with its practices and have put on the new self. You are being renewed in knowledge according to the image of your Creator. In Christ there is not Greek and Jew, circumcision and uncircumcision, barbarian, Scythian, slave and free; but Christ is all and in all."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
The pivot hinges on two words: "but now." Yesterday's vices — lust, greed, idolatry — were sins of the body's appetites. Today Paul moves to sins of the tongue and the heart's social posture: anger, wrath, malice, slander, filthy speech, and lying. These are relational sins, the ones that tear communities apart from the inside. The Greek word for "anger" here is orgē — a settled, brooding disposition, as opposed to the flash of thumos (wrath). Both are addressed. "Malice" (kakia) is ill-will toward others — wanting bad things for people. "Slander" (blasphēmia) means speech that tears down a person's reputation. Paul names them precisely because the community at Colossae would have recognized them. These weren't theoretical sins — they were the daily friction of people from radically different backgrounds trying to live together. The solution isn't tolerance or better conflict resolution. It is the complete stripping off of the old self and its entire way of operating.
Colossians 3:8
☩ Open in Bible App
"But now you must also put away all the following: anger, wrath, malice, slander, and abusive language from your mouth."
English Standard Version · ESV
"But now, put away all the following: anger, wrath, malice, slander, and filthy language from your mouth."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
The ESV's "abusive language" captures something the CSB's "filthy language" partly obscures. The Greek is aischrologia — speech that is shameful, degrading, designed to humiliate. It's not just crude language in general; it's language used as a weapon against another person's dignity. Notice the list moves inward to outward: anger (the internal disposition) produces wrath (its expression), which produces malice (active ill-will), which spills into slander and finally into abusive speech. The escalation is intentional. Paul is describing how unchecked internal states inevitably corrupt everything they touch on the way out.
Mark 3:5
☩ Open in Bible App
"After looking around at them with anger, he was grieved at the hardness of their hearts and told the man, 'Stretch out your hand.' So he stretched it out, and his hand was restored."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
This verse does something crucial for understanding the prohibition on anger in Colossians: it shows that anger itself is not sin. Jesus looked at the Pharisees with orgē — the same word Paul uses in Colossians 3:8 — and it was entirely righteous. The difference? Jesus's anger was paired with grief (syllypoumenos — a deep, empathetic sorrow for the people being harmed by religious rigidity). His anger was directed at injustice, not at the people themselves as enemies. Human anger almost never operates this cleanly. We are angry for mixed reasons, with mixed motives, and without the perfect knowledge needed to be purely righteous in it. James 1:19–20 makes the practical conclusion plain: "the wrath of man does not produce the righteousness of God." The anger Paul forbids in Colossians is the human variety — self-serving, self-righteous, ungrieved, and destructive.
James 1:19–20
☩ Open in Bible App
"So then, my beloved brethren, let every man be swift to hear, slow to speak, slow to wrath; for the wrath of man does not produce the righteousness of God."
New King James Version · NKJV
"My dear brothers and sisters, understand this: Everyone should be quick to listen, slow to speak, and slow to anger, for human anger does not accomplish God's righteousness."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
James is not saying that anger always leads to sin — he is saying it rarely produces righteousness. The symmetry of "swift to hear, slow to speak, slow to wrath" is deliberate. Listening first is an act of humility. Speaking slowly is an act of wisdom. Delaying anger is an act of trust — trusting that God's justice doesn't need our fury to get things right. When anger does speak, it tends to speak past the real problem and create new ones. James's point reinforces Paul's: what belongs to the old self is not just the acts of anger but the entire reflexive posture of react-first, listen-never.
Colossians 3:9
☩ Open in Bible App
"Do not lie to one another, since you have put off the old man with his deeds."
New King James Version · NKJV
"Do not lie to one another, since you have put off the old self with its practices."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
The NKJV retains the literal Greek "old man" (palaios anthrōpos) — a more vivid image than the CSB's "old self." Paul isn't describing a personality trait to be managed. He is describing an entire person: an identity, a way of being in the world, a complete set of social habits and relational patterns. That whole person has been put off — stripped off, like a filthy garment. The prohibition against lying (Leviticus 19:11 makes clear this has always been a boundary of covenant community) is the specific application, but the ground beneath it is the total replacement of the old identity. You don't lie not merely because lying is wrong, but because the person who lies to protect himself is the old man — and he is gone.
Romans 6:6
☩ Open in Bible App
"For we know that our old self was crucified with him so that the body ruled by sin might be rendered powerless so that we may no longer be enslaved to sin."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Here Paul answers the question that naturally follows Colossians 3:9: if the old man has been "put off," what actually happened to him? This verse is the theological ground beneath the clothing metaphor. The old self wasn't improved, or put in timeout, or disciplined into better behavior. It was crucified — executed — with Christ on the cross. The Greek word translated "rendered powerless" is katargeō, meaning to make inoperative, to nullify, to put out of business. Sin's operating system — the entire framework through which the old self functioned — has been disabled. This doesn't mean the impulses are gone (Paul will address that directly in Romans 7). It means the authority is gone. The old self no longer has legal claim on us. What we do now with our bodies and our words is a choice made from freedom, not a compulsion made from slavery.
Ephesians 4:22–24
☩ Open in Bible App
"to take off your former way of life, the old self that is corrupted by deceitful desires, to be renewed in the spirit of your minds, and to put on the new self, the one created according to God's likeness in righteousness and purity of the truth."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
The same clothing metaphor appears here in Ephesians with an additional layer: the old self is being actively corrupted — the Greek phtheiromenon suggests something decaying, rotting, wasting away. It is not static. Left unchallenged, the old self doesn't stay in place — it deteriorates and drags everything around it down with it. The renewal Paul describes is equally active. "To be renewed in the spirit of your minds" is an ongoing process, not a one-time event. The new self, by contrast, is described as having been created (ktisthenta — aorist, completed action) according to God's own likeness. The old self is a deteriorating copy of something that was never properly made. The new self is God's own workmanship, shaped from the start toward his own character.
2 Corinthians 3:18
☩ Open in Bible App
"We all, with unveiled faces, are looking as in a mirror at the glory of the Lord and are being transformed into the same image from glory to glory; this is from the Lord who is the Spirit."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
This is one of the most compact and beautiful descriptions of spiritual formation in all of Paul's letters. The contrast is with Moses, who had to veil his face because Israel couldn't bear the reflected glory of God after he met with him at Sinai. But those in Christ stand before God unveiled — because the veil of sin and death has been removed. The Greek word translated "looking as in a mirror" is katoptrizomenoi, which carries the double sense of both beholding and reflecting. We gaze at Christ and, in gazing, we begin to show what we see. The transformation is described as "from glory to glory" — an ascending progression, not a plateau. And crucially, Paul says the agent of this change is the Spirit of the Lord. This is not willpower. It is not a self-improvement program. It is what happens when a person is genuinely and continuously in Christ's presence.
2 Corinthians 5:17
☩ Open in Bible App
"Therefore, if anyone is in Christ, he is a new creation; old things have passed away; behold, all things have become new."
New King James Version · NKJV
"Therefore, if anyone is in Christ, he is a new creation; the old has passed away, and see, the new has come!"
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
The NKJV's "old things have passed away; behold, all things have become new" has an exclamatory quality the CSB matches with its "see, the new has come!" — both translations trying to capture the surprise and totality Paul intends. The word "creation" here is ktisis — the same word used for the original act of creation in Genesis. Paul is not describing renovation. He is describing a new-creation event. The same power that spoke the universe into existence has spoken a new person into existence in the believer. The old categories, the old loyalties, the old damage — they belong to a world that, from the vantage point of being "in Christ," has already passed away.
Colossians 3:10
☩ Open in Bible App
"and have put on the new man who is being renewed in knowledge according to the image of Him who created him."
New King James Version · NKJV
"and have put on the new self. You are being renewed in knowledge according to the image of your Creator."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
The phrase "renewed in knowledge" is the theological heart of today's study. The Greek is anakainoumenon eis epignōsin — renewed unto full, precise, experiential knowledge. This isn't the accumulation of Bible facts. It is the kind of knowing that transforms the knower — a deep, personal encounter with the character of God as revealed in Christ. The standard of this renewal is "the image of him who created him." Paul is pointing back to Genesis 1:26–27, where human beings were made in the imago Dei — the image of God. That image was distorted by sin. The new self is not just a repaired version of the old one; it is humanity restarting the project of bearing God's image, this time from a foundation that cannot be corrupted.
Colossians 1:15
☩ Open in Bible App
"He is the image of the invisible God, the firstborn over all creation."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
Paul planted this verse earlier in the letter and is now drawing on it directly. Christ is the eikōn — the exact image, the perfect and complete representation — of the invisible God. When Paul says the new self is being renewed "according to the image of your Creator," he means according to the image as it has been fully and finally revealed in Jesus. We are not being conformed to an abstract ideal of holiness. We are being conformed to a person. The target of our transformation has a face, a history, a set of specific words and actions — and his name is Jesus. This is why knowing Christ through his Word matters so much: you cannot be transformed into the image of someone you never look at.
Colossians 3:11
☩ Open in Bible App
"In Christ there is not Greek and Jew, circumcision and uncircumcision, barbarian, Scythian, slave and free; but Christ is all and in all."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
The climax of the passage lands here, and it lands like a thunderclap in its original context. Paul runs through the most charged social divisions of his world: ethnic (Greek and Jew), religious (circumcised and uncircumcised), cultural (barbarian), and what was considered the absolute bottom of civilization (Scythian — the ancient world's shorthand for savage, beyond the reach of law or culture), and finally economic (slave and free). All of it, Paul says, is simply not operative "in Christ." This is the logical conclusion of the new creation. If the old self has been crucified and replaced with a new self shaped in God's image, then the social identities that belonged to the old self have no ultimate claim either. The new community is not colorblind or culture-erasing — it is a community where those categories no longer determine rank, worth, or standing. Christ is all the identity that matters, and he is "in all."
🎯 Street Level
A Factory Reset That Actually Works

When a phone is seriously broken — not just slow, but corrupted at the operating system level — you don't fix it by downloading a better app. You do a factory reset. The entire OS is wiped and reinstalled fresh from the manufacturer's original image.

Paul's "old self / new self" isn't a patch update. It's a factory reset at the level of personhood. The old operating system — the one that ran anger, self-protection, deception, and tribalism as background processes — has been wiped. The new self is being reinstalled according to the original image: the image of God as revealed in Christ. The catch is that a factory reset doesn't mean you suddenly know how to use the phone differently. You still have to learn new habits, deliberately use new features, and stop reflexively reaching for the old ones. That's what "being renewed in knowledge" looks like in practice — it's the daily process of learning to operate from the new OS instead of the one that no longer exists.

🌍 Today's World, Ancient Truth
Tribalism Is the Operating System of Our Moment

Researchers studying political psychology have documented what many people already feel: we have become remarkably good at identifying and despising the "other" group, and remarkably bad at humanizing them. Studies show that Americans increasingly dislike, distrust, and even dehumanize people from the opposing political party more than they dislike members of any racial or religious out-group. The categories have changed from Paul's day — no one uses "Scythian" anymore — but the underlying human impulse to sort, rank, and diminish is identical.

Churches are not immune. Congregations increasingly mirror the political and cultural sorting of the broader society, and the relational sins Paul names in Colossians 3:8 — slander, malice, abusive speech — now have unprecedented reach through social media. What would have taken years to spread through a community in first-century Colossae takes seconds today.

Paul's word to the Colossians is the word the church most needs to hear now: the divisions that feel so real, so important, so worth fighting about — they belong to the old self. And the old self has been crucified. The new community is defined by a different center entirely. "Christ is all and in all" is either the most transformative social vision in history, or it's just words. The church's witness depends on which one it actually believes.

✝️ The Christ Connection

The new self is not a spiritual aspiration. It is a person — Jesus Christ. When Paul says we are being renewed "according to the image of your Creator," and then points to Christ as "the image of the invisible God," he is closing a loop: the target of our transformation is the man who walked out of a tomb.

This matters enormously. Jesus didn't just teach us to love our enemies — he refused to slander them even while being executed by them. He didn't just counsel against anger — he wept over Jerusalem rather than raged at it. He didn't just preach unity — he ate with tax collectors and Pharisees and fishermen and women with scandalous histories, all at the same table. The new self Paul describes in Colossians 3 is not an ethical ideal. It is the character of Christ himself, being reproduced in his people by the Spirit.

And the cross is where the old self was put to death so this could happen. Romans 6:6 says our old man was crucified with him. The violence of the cross wasn't just Christ absorbing God's wrath for our sins — it was the execution of everything we were without him. What rose on the third day was new. And that newness is what we have been invited into.

✅ Personal Application Challenge
Identify one person in your life toward whom you carry a settled negative disposition — a low-grade orgē, a quiet contempt, a habitual defensiveness. Today, before you interact with them (or before you scroll past their name), pray specifically for them: name one thing you genuinely hope goes well for them. Then notice whether it changes how you think about them even slightly. This is what it looks like to put off the old man one repetition at a time.
🔁 Connection to Yesterday

Yesterday we looked at the sins Paul calls "earthly" in Colossians 3:5 — the appetites: sexual immorality, impurity, lust, evil desire, and greed. The thread running between yesterday and today is the same two-word pivot: "but now." Yesterday's study established that God's wrath falls on these behaviors because they represent a life still ordered around self and flesh rather than Christ. Today Paul moves from sins of the body's appetites to sins of the community's atmosphere — anger, slander, deception. Yesterday was about what we do alone. Today is about what we do to each other. Together, they form a complete picture of the old self: disordered inwardly, and destructive outwardly. Both must go. And both are addressed not by willpower but by the same answer: a new self, shaped in the image of the One who made us.

🙏 Closing Prayer

Lord, I am more aware than I'd like to be of how much of the old self still operates in me — the quick defensiveness, the slow suspicion, the words I choose when I'm hurt. Thank you that Paul doesn't call me to try harder. He calls me to remember what is already true: the old man was crucified. He does not have authority over me anymore. Help me live like that is real today. Renew me in the knowledge of who you actually are — not the version I've constructed, not the version that conveniently agrees with all my existing opinions, but the Christ who refused to slander his executioners and wept over a city that rejected him. Shape me into that image. And where I carry bitterness toward people in categories I've placed them in — political, cultural, personal — remind me that in you, those categories are finished. You are all and in all. In Jesus' name, Amen.
