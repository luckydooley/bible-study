Monday, End Earthliness

Colossians · Week 11, Day 3
March 9, 2026
✦ ☩ ✦
📌 TL;DR

You can't live toward heaven while still feeding the habits that belong to a life you've already died out of.

  • ☩ Resurrection identity demands resurrection behavior: Paul's command to "put to death" earthly desires is the natural consequence of being raised with Christ.
  • ☩ The tension is real: our bodies still carry old appetites even though spiritually we have already died to them.
  • ☩ Paul's list of earthly "members" covers the full range of ways we can cling to the old life, from sexual immorality to covetousness, and names them all as a form of idolatry.
  • ☩ God's wrath is not arbitrary punishment but the inevitable outcome of choosing the creature over the Creator, and the call today is to choose differently.
🗺️ Historical & Cultural Context
Colossae Was Saturated with Competing Desires

The city of Colossae sat at a crossroads in the Lycus Valley, and its population was exactly the kind of mixed, pluralistic community Paul was writing into: Jewish diaspora, Gentile converts, former devotees of the mystery religions, and people steeped in the sexual ethics of the Greco-Roman world. For most of those converts, the body was either something to be indulged freely (because the spirit was all that mattered) or something to be punished through asceticism (because matter was inherently evil). Both errors were alive in the Colossian church.

Paul refuses both escape routes. He doesn't say the body is evil, nor does he say it doesn't matter. He says the body is the very site where resurrection life either gets expressed or betrayed. The earthly desires he lists in Colossians 3:5 were not abstract vices. They were the daily social currency of Colossian life: sexual looseness was normalized in Roman culture, covetousness was baked into the patronage economy, and the blending of worship with sensual indulgence was a feature of multiple local cults. Paul's readers would have heard this list as a direct inventory of the world they had come out of, and were still surrounded by.

📖 Into the Word

The anchor passage today is Colossians 3:5–6, the direct consequence of everything Paul established in verses 1–4. Because believers have died and been raised with Christ, they are now summoned to live accordingly: actively putting to death the desires that belong to the old life. Colossians 3:1 makes clear why this command carries such weight, and Romans 1:18–27 describes the sobering trajectory of a life that refuses it. Romans 6:1–7 then gives the liberating counter-truth: death with Christ is not merely a metaphor but a real transfer of allegiance, and the freedom to mortify earthly things flows from that transfer.

Colossians 3:5–6
☩ Open in Bible App
"Therefore, put to death what belongs to your earthly nature: sexual immorality, impurity, lust, evil desire, and greed, which is idolatry. Because of these, God's wrath comes on the disobedient."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
The Greek word here is nekrōsate (νεκρώσατε), from nekroō, meaning "to put to death." This is not a suggestion to gradually reduce bad habits. It is a commanding, urgent aorist imperative: kill it, decisively and completely. The phrase "your earthly nature" translates ta melē ta epi tēs gēs, literally "your members that are upon the earth," referring not to the physical body itself but to its appetites and impulses when directed away from God. Paul lists five specific sins: porneian (sexual immorality, the broadest term for sexual sin outside of God's design), akatharsia (impurity, moral uncleanness in thought, word, or act), pathos (lust, degrading passion), epithymian kakēn (evil desire, vehement craving for what is wrong), and pleonexian (greed, the insatiable wanting of more). That last one Paul equates directly with idolatry. Covetousness is not merely a moral failing; it is the replacement of God with the thing desired. When you must have something more than you need God, that thing has become your god.
Colossians 3:1
☩ Open in Bible App
"So if you have been raised with Christ, seek the things above, where Christ is, seated at the right hand of God."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
The Greek word for "members" in verse 5 (melē) appears here in its theological context. Paul's command to mortify earthly members is a consequence of the fact stated in verse 1: you have been raised with Christ. The "therefore" of verse 5 points directly back to this. Because resurrection is your new identity, the appetites that belong to the old life no longer have a legitimate claim on you. The command is not the foundation; this verse is. Behavior flows from identity, not the other way around.
Romans 1:18
☩ Open in Bible App
"For God's wrath is revealed from heaven against all godlessness and unrighteousness of people who by their unrighteousness suppress the truth."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
This verse illuminates Colossians 3:6's warning that "God's wrath comes on the disobedient." The Greek word for "wrath" is orgē (ὀργή), a settled, righteous indignation against evil rather than an explosive rage. Crucially, the wrath is already being "revealed," present tense, not merely threatened for a future day. Paul connects "uncleanness" in Colossians 3:5 directly to the "ungodliness and unrighteousness" named here. These are not separate categories; they are the same disorder seen from different angles.
Romans 1:24
☩ Open in Bible App
"Therefore God delivered them over in the desires of their hearts to sexual impurity, so that their bodies were degraded among themselves."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
The phrase "God delivered them over" translates paredōken autous ho theos (παρέδωκεν αὐτοὺς ὁ θεός), a judicial act: God hands people over to the full consequences of what they have chosen. This is not God abandoning people in anger; it is God honoring their choice by removing restraint and allowing the trajectory of that choice to run its course. The connection to Colossians 3:5 is direct: the "vile passions" of Romans 1 are precisely what Paul commands believers to kill before that process begins.
Romans 1:26–27
☩ Open in Bible App
"For this reason God delivered them over to disgraceful passions. Their women exchanged natural sexual relations for unnatural ones. The men in the same way also left natural relations with women and were inflamed in their lust for one another. Men committed shameless acts with men and received in themselves the appropriate penalty of their error."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
"God delivered them over" appears a second time, deepening the pattern established in verse 24. Paul is not singling out one sin above others; he is illustrating the progressive unraveling that follows when the Creator is exchanged for the creature. Every item on the Colossians 3:5 list can be traced back to this root: when God is no longer the center, something else rushes in to fill that space, and the disorder spreads outward from there. The Greek word atimias (disgraceful, shameful) carries the sense of something that dishonors what was made to bear the image of God.
Romans 6:1–7
☩ Open in Bible App
"What should we say then? Should we continue in sin so that grace may multiply? Absolutely not! How can we who died to sin still live in it? Or are you unaware that all of us who were baptized into Christ Jesus were baptized into his death? Therefore we were buried with him by baptism into death, in order that, just as Christ was raised from the dead by the glory of the Father, so we too may walk in newness of life. For if we have been united with him in the likeness of his death, we will certainly also be in the likeness of his resurrection. For we know that our old self was crucified with him so that the body ruled by sin might be rendered powerless so that we may no longer be enslaved to sin, since a person who has died is freed from sin."
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
This passage answers the question Romans 1 raises: if that is what unchecked earthliness looks like, how does anyone escape it? The answer is death and resurrection with Christ. Baptism is a participation in both: the old self is crucified, the new self is raised. The Greek word for "freed" in verse 7 is dedikaiōtai (δεδικαίωται), the same word translated "justified" elsewhere in Romans, meaning death with Christ brings a legal acquittal from sin's dominion. This is why the command to mortify earthly desires is not grinding moralism. It is an invitation to live consistently with a liberation that has already occurred. You don't put earthly desires to death in order to be free; you put them to death because you already are.
Revelation 6:16–17
☩ Open in Bible App
"And they said to the mountains and to the rocks, 'Fall on us and hide us from the face of the One seated on the throne and from the wrath of the Lamb, because the great day of Their wrath has come! And who is able to stand?'"
Christian Standard Bible · CSB
The word "Lamb" is arresting. Lambs are associated with meekness and sacrifice, not judgment. Yet Revelation makes clear that the same Christ who absorbed wrath on the cross will one day bring its final resolution. The question "Who is able to stand?" gives Colossians 3:6's warning its full eschatological weight: the wrath Paul mentions is not merely a present-tense social consequence but has a final dimension. This is not meant to terrify but to orient. The daily choices we make about earthly desires are not trivial. They are the rehearsal of where we place our ultimate allegiance.
🎯 Street Level
The Account That Still Has Your Old Card Saved

You cancel a subscription. You close the account. But months later you discover the old card is still saved, and the platform has been quietly charging you the whole time. You thought it was done, but the connection was never fully severed.

That's the picture Paul is working with. Baptism, conversion, the decision to follow Christ: these are real endings. The old account is closed. But the old card, meaning the old appetites, the old habits, the old reflexes, is still saved in the system. "Put to death your earthly members" is the work of going back in and actually deleting it. Not suppressing it. Not managing it. Removing the connection entirely so it can no longer quietly charge you.

🌍 Today's World, Ancient Truth
Dopamine Culture Is Just Covetousness with Better Branding

The last several years have seen a wave of cultural reckoning with what researchers are calling the "attention economy," the deliberate engineering of digital platforms to trigger desire and keep users in a state of perpetual want. Social media feeds are designed to surface what you almost have, the lifestyle just out of reach, the product you didn't know you needed, the version of yourself that would exist if you just acquired one more thing. Psychologists have identified this as a driver of rising anxiety, comparison, and what they're now calling "scroll dissatisfaction," a chronic low-grade sense that whatever you currently have isn't enough.

Paul named this dynamic two thousand years ago. Pleonexia, the Greek word he uses for greed in Colossians 3:5, literally means "the desire for more." It is the condition of always needing the next thing in order to feel complete. And Paul calls it idolatry, not because wanting things is evil, but because when the wanting becomes unmoored from God as the source of satisfaction, it inevitably fills that space with something else. The attention economy is a sophisticated industrial-scale machine for manufacturing pleonexia. Paul's prescription, putting it to death, is as countercultural today as it was in Colossae.

✝️ The Christ Connection

The command to mortify earthly desires only makes sense because of what Christ already did. He didn't just model self-denial; he absorbed the full weight of the wrath that belongs to those who refuse it. Romans 5:8 says that while we were still sinners, Christ died for us. The wrath of the Lamb described in Revelation 6 is real, and it has already fallen somewhere: on the cross. Every desire we are called to put to death was nailed there first.

This is why Paul's command is not grinding moralism. It is a participation in something already accomplished. We put earthly things to death because Christ put our death to death. The freedom to say no to the old life is not willpower; it is the fruit of the resurrection. The same power that raised Jesus from the dead is the power at work in believers who choose, one act at a time, to live from their new identity rather than their old appetites.

✅ Personal Application Challenge
Look at Paul's list in Colossians 3:5 and name one specific item on it that still has a live connection in your life, not in general terms but concretely. Then take one practical step today to cut that connection: delete the app, end the habit, have the conversation, confess it to someone you trust. One specific thing. Not a plan to eventually deal with it. Today.
🔁 Connection to Yesterday

Yesterday's study on heavenly-mindedness established that the foundation of Christian living is identity, not effort. Because you have been raised with Christ, you set your mind on things above. Today's passage is the direct consequence of that. Paul moves without pause from "your life is hidden with Christ in God" to "therefore, put to death your earthly members." The indicative drives the imperative. You don't mortify earthly desires in order to become someone who has died and risen with Christ. You mortify them because you already are that person. Sunday was the root. Monday is the fruit.

🙏 Closing Prayer

Lord, the honest truth is that putting things to death is hard. The old appetites don't go quietly, and I'm better at managing them than killing them. But today's passage doesn't give me the option of a slow negotiated peace with sin. It says kill it. So I'm asking for the same power that raised Jesus to be at work in me today, not in some vague future version of myself but right now. Help me to see clearly which "member" still has a live connection to the old life, and give me the courage to actually cut it rather than just intend to. Thank you that the wrath I deserve already fell on someone else. Let that reality be what moves me today. In Jesus' name, Amen.
